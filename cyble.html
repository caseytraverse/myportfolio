overview

 I was tasked with researching and identifying solutions that would benefit the mobility challenged community and bring a new, unique accessibility application to the market.

the problem

Technology can be used to enhance and improve the lives of countless people across the world. This makes it so surprising that there is such a void of accessibility solutions on the market. My role in this project was to create a navigation app to locate accessible restrooms, parking, and more.

the solution

Cyble is an all-in-one accessibility navigation application. Users can locate and rate appropriate restrooms, parking, establishments, or more. This application provides a one-stop source to benefit those with mobility challenges specifically designed to enhance their experience while they’re traveling or on the go.

design process

User Research

I began by collecting data from over 60 key demographic user survey participants. This allowed me to begin to identify certain patterns, as well as differentiators from the user research gathered. A few particular interesting pieces of information compiled were:

  • For those who answered that they have not used an accessibility app or website 53.49% of them said the main reason was that they didn’t know that accessibility apps had existed.
  • 88.4% of these respondents said that a particular feature could be added to an accessibility app that would make it enticing enough for them to start using an accessibility app/website.
  • The leading features that could be enticing enough for participants to start using an accessibility app are including an accessible bathroom locator (39.47%), parking locator (23.68%), and implementing a rating/review system (13.16%).

In the initial stage of building, the concept began as a handicap accessibility app that would allow users to review the accessibility of restaurants, movie theaters, parks, venues, museums, malls, and more. After the research data was considered, the concept matured to also include - more importantly, locating accessible restrooms and parking.

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive analysis on the handicap accessibility marketplace analyzing Wheelmap, AccessAble and Google Maps. While all three do offer relevant accessibility information, not one of these competitors offers a feature that allows the user to specifically locate accessible restrooms or parking.

Wheelmap. AccessAble. Google Maps.

According to survey data, locating accessible restrooms or parking were the top two key leading features that survey participants said that could be enticing enough for them to start using an accessibility app. Through this analysis, I was able to recognize a weakness in the market which presented itself as an opportunity for me to create unique desirable features for this application.

User Persona's

I created the following user personas:

Elizabeth Thayer

Age: 34

Tier: Frequent

Location: Phoenix, Arizona

Occupation: Communications Specialist

Role: Access, Research, Review

Motivations:

Liz’s horseback riding instructor introduced her to an app called Wheelmap. She loves using the app when she's out and about to help her find wheelchair accessible places in her area. She finds it extremely helpful in her day to day life, her wish, however, was that the app had more data on businesses in her immediate area.

Goals:

  • Accurate and detailed information
  • Accessible restroom and parking locator
  • Ratings & reviews from others with accessibility challenges

Frustrations:

  • Not enough data on local businesses
  • Little to no reviews or ratings in her immediate area
  • Does not offer real-time information such as if an elevator is down

Bio:

Elizabeth Thayer is a 34-year-old daughter, mother, and wife. She works as a Communications Specialist for Phoenix Arizona Hospital. She enjoys reading, swimming, horseback riding, and spending time with her family. Liz wishes there was an app to tell her where the closest restrooms and accessible parking was - she would feel more comfortable visiting new unfamiliar destinations if she had access to this information.

Quotes:

“I go to places that I know that I will be accommodating to me and my wheelchair”

Branding

Mindmapping exercise

One of the problems users faced when traveling was locating accessible restrooms and parking. I wanted to induce a sense of trust and confidence in the platform. I came up with five core brand objectives:

  • Usable
  • Capable
  • Available
  • Accessible
  • Obtainable

In line with my brand objectives, I created a cool color palette. Blue is used heavily throughout the design for its calming, concentrative and communicative properties. Green is used as it is a color that strikes the eye without the need to require adjustment - it is considered a restful color. Lastly, purple is sparingly used which holds association with respect, authenticity, truth and quality.

Color palette from styleguide

Logo

The logo design combines inspiration from the International Symbol of Access (ISA, or also known as the International Wheelchair Symbol) and a common GPS current location identifier circle.

The logo originates from the brand’s vision to solve handicapped user’s accessibility challenges, such as locating appropriate restrooms, parking, and more. The boldness of this logo is visually distinct and recognizable at various sizes.

Initial logo sketch

Final logo design

Typography

To determine the typeface for the brand I had to consider a lot more than aesthetics and styling. My consideration had to be made for accessibility and readability first and foremost. With this in mind I decided on the sans-serif font Arial. The sans-serif’s friendly and open curves allow for easy reading on screens of various sizes. Additionally, being one of the most legible and accessible fonts widely available, I determined this was the best choice.

Prototype v1.0

When creating my hi-fi mockups, I incorporated the selected styling and branding to accentuate the design.

One of the biggest challenges during the visual design process was to ensure that all elements were compliant to accessibility standards (this included color, sizing, spacing, typography, and contrast ratio).

Prototype version 1.0

User Testing

For the usability tests, I had users complete the following 5 tasks:

  • Forgot password
  • Signing up for an account
  • Rating an establishments accessibility
  • Locating accessible restaurants and parking
  • Logging out of the account

I also asked users to comment on the brand and overall look of the app. How did it make them feel? What was the brand conveying to them?

Overall, most users were able to complete all of the tasks. They also commented that they felt the app looked very clean, crisp, and intuitive. However, the app was far from being complete. Users were able to point out certain improvements that I may not have otherwise noticed.

Reflection

This project was really special for me. It was extremely gratifying designing a product that could enhance the user’s lives by offering solutions to make their world more accessible. I paid particular attention to keeping the users in mind during development. With their input throughout the design process, I was able to make fundamental improvements to my initial ideas. By listening to the users' concerns and problems, I was able to create something that would be effective in making their lives a little more accessible.
